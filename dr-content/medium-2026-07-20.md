# How to Ship from Taiwan to China in 2026: The Complete Guide Nobody Writes

*By A-Jie | 6 min read*

If you've ever stood at a Chunghwa Post counter holding a box of pineapple cakes, only to hear "sorry, food items cannot be shipped to China," you know the frustration. Taiwan-to-China shipping is a maze of contradictory rules, hidden fees, and carrier restrictions that most guides gloss over.

I've spent the last 5 years shipping everything from tea leaves to laptops across the strait. Here's what actually works in 2026.

## The 3 Routes: What They Don't Tell You

### Route 1: Chunghwa Post (中華郵政)
**Best for:** Documents, clothes, books — nothing edible or electronic.

The official option. Nice if your parcel contains exactly what they allow. But the moment you try to ship tea, supplements, cosmetics, or anything with a battery, the counter staff will flatly refuse. Even if they accept it, your recipient might get hit with a customs bill they didn't expect.

- **Cost:** NT$300–800 for 1-2kg
- **Transit:** 14–30 days (air parcel) or 30–60 days (surface)
- **Tax:** NOT included. Recipient bears all import duties.

### Route 2: Commercial Couriers (SF Express, DHL)
**Best for:** Documents, commercial samples, high-value items with proper invoices.

They're fast. They're professional. And they're completely unforgiving about prohibited items. SF Express will reject food and supplements outright because their customs clearance process demands full commercial invoices and strict compliance. DHL is even stricter — and pricier.

- **Cost:** NT$1,200+ for 1kg via SF Express
- **Transit:** 3–5 working days
- **Hidden cost:** If customs flags your parcel, you pay storage fees while sorting it out.

### Route 3: Specialty Cross-Strait Forwarders
**Best for:** Food, supplements, cosmetics, electronics, tea — basically everything the post office rejects.

This is the route most people don't know about. Specialty forwarders have dedicated customs channels for "sensitive goods" (敏感貨). They handle the paperwork, absorb the customs risk, and often offer tax-inclusive (包稅) pricing. The trade-off: you need to do some homework to find a reliable one.

- **Cost:** NT$290/kg (general sensitive goods), NT$350/kg (electronics with batteries)
- **Transit:** 5–7 working days (air) or 7–12 days (economy)
- **Tax:** Usually included in the rate (包稅)

## What You Can (and Can't) Ship

This is where most guides give dangerously outdated information. Here's the 2026 reality:

**✅ Can Ship (via specialty forwarder):**
- Factory-sealed snacks and food (pineapple cakes, nougat, tea, instant noodles without meat chunks)
- Health supplements (fish oil, vitamins, collagen)
- Cosmetics and skincare (sheet masks, serums, perfumes)
- Electronics with batteries (phones, laptops, tablets, Bluetooth earphones)
- Clothing, books, toys, small appliances

**❌ Cannot Ship (period):**
- Fresh meat, fresh fruit, fresh seafood
- Loose tea leaves without factory packaging
- Individual lithium batteries (UN3480 — must be installed in device)
- Meat-containing mooncakes, beef jerky, dried pork
- Drones (case-by-case, battery must be removable)

**⚠️ Gray Area (ask first):**
- Traditional Chinese medicine (herbs OK, animal-derived ingredients not OK)
- Second-hand electronics (may require repair purpose documentation)
- Large appliances (dimensional weight may make it uneconomical)

## The Customs Trap Nobody Warns About

In 2026, mainland China customs tightened inspection on meat-containing products. If your instant noodles have actual beef chunks (not just beef flavoring), they will get flagged. The penalty isn't just confiscation — your entire parcel gets held, and the forwarder's customs license takes a hit.

**Golden rule:** If it once had a pulse, don't ship it. Stick to factory-sealed, shelf-stable, clearly labeled products.

## How to Choose a Forwarder

I've tested multiple forwarders over the years. Here's my checklist:

1. **Line/WeChat responsiveness** — Do they reply in under 30 minutes? If not, skip.
2. **Transparent pricing** — If they can't give you a per-kg rate upfront, they're hiding fees.
3. **Tax-inclusive (包稅)** — Essential for peace of mind. Make sure it's in writing.
4. **Tracking** — You should get a tracking number that works on both Taiwan and China sides.
5. **Physical office** — A legitimate forwarder has a verifiable Taiwan business address.
